The Messier Catalog

A comet-hunter’s “not comets” list became the most famous catalog of deep-sky objects in astronomy.

In 18th-century France, an astronomer named Charles Messier (1730–1817) spent his nights hunting comets. Comets meant glory — first sightings were rewarded, named, and remembered. But the night sky is full of fuzzy patches that look like comets and aren’t. They don’t move from night to night. To save himself the embarrassment of mistaking one for a comet, Messier began writing them down: a list of not-comets to skip.

That list became the gold-standard target list for amateur astronomers for the next 250 years. Messier wasn’t trying to catalog deep-sky objects — he was trying to ignore them.

What’s in the Catalog

Messier published his first version in 1774 and his expanded final list of 103 objects in 1781. Astronomers later added M104 through M110 based on Messier’s own observing records. The full modern list spans every major class of deep-sky object visible from northern mid-latitudes:

Galaxies — M31 (Andromeda), M51 (Whirlpool), M81 / M82 (Bode’s Galaxy and the Cigar), M101 (Pinwheel), M104 (Sombrero), and the entire Virgo Cluster sprawl from M84 through M100.

Bright nebulae — M1 (Crab supernova remnant), M8 (Lagoon), M16 (Eagle — the “Pillars of Creation”), M17 (Omega), M20 (Trifid), M27 (Dumbbell), M42 (the Orion Nebula), M57 (Ring), M97 (Owl).

Open clusters — M6 (Butterfly), M7 (Ptolemy Cluster), M11 (Wild Duck), M44 (Beehive), and the iconic M45 (Pleiades).

Globular clusters — M3, M13 (the Great Hercules Cluster), M22, M55, M92, and many others — ancient bound swarms of hundreds of thousands of stars.

Curiosities — M40 (a double star, almost certainly a Messier mistake), M45 (technically a naked-eye asterism rather than a clustered DSO), M73 (an asterism of four stars).

The Messier Marathon

For a few weeks each spring — around the new moon nearest the vernal equinox — an observer at the right latitude can race through every Messier object in a single night. Setting wake-up alarms, choosing efficient sequences, beating the dawn. This is the Messier Marathon, an unofficial endurance contest among amateur astronomers and a rite of passage for many.

Tracking Yours in Moondance

The Moondance app includes a Messier Tracker built into the Sky Map and constellation views. Activate the Messier filter pill and the dome highlights only Messier objects, with a running label showing your capture progress: Messier 34 / 108, then 35, 36…

Tap any constellation to see its Messier objects ordered by Messier number, each with a star icon you can flip to mark the object captured. The capture is date-stamped and persists across your sessions. Over time you build a personal record of every Messier you’ve imaged.

“Show all other Messier objects” on a constellation page reveals every Messier across all other constellations, grouped by host constellation, so you can plan your run from any single starting point.
